3.2. Sting Location Analysis These data come from [Smith, 2014] where the author experimented on himself by daily stinging himself five times on randomly selected body locations over the course of months. You can read more about this fascinating (and cringe inducing) study at https://peerj.com/articles/338/. The following code gets the data prepared for analysis by removing the observations he took each day on how painful it was to sting himself on his forearm before and after the other three observations that were of interest each day of the study. This is done with a negation (using \(!\) of the %in% which identifies rows related to the two daily forearm locations (Forearm and Forearm1) to leave all the rows in the data set for any levels of Body_Location that were not in these two levels. This is easier than trying to list all 24 other levels, then Body_Location variable is re-factored to clean out its unused levels, and finally the reorder function is used to order the levels based on the sample mean pain rating and the results of these steps are stored in the sd_fixedR tibble.

sd_fixed <- read_csv("http://www.math.montana.edu/courses/s217/documents/stingdata_fixed.csv")
Rows: 120 Columns: 7 Column specification 
Delimiter: ","
chr  (4): Body_Location, Side, Date, Round
dbl  (2): Rating, OrderinDay
time (1): Time
 Use `spec()` to retrieve the full column specification for this data.
 Specify the column types or set `show_col_types = FALSE` to quiet this message.
sd_fixedR <- sd_fixed %>%
  filter(!(Body_Location %in% c("Forearm", "Forearm1"))) %>%
  mutate(Body_Location = factor(Body_Location),
         Body_Location = reorder(Body_Location, Rating, FUN = mean))

3.2.3. How does taking 3 measurements that are of interest each day lead to a violation of the independence assumption here?

Collecting 3 measurements of interest each day may violate the independence assumption since measurements within the same day could be influenced by common daily factors, compromising the randomness and independence required for accurate statistical analysis.

For the scope of inference use the information that the sting locations were randomly assigned but only one person (the researcher) participated in the study.

  1. Hypotheses: \(H_0\): \(\mu\) (same for all body parts) \(H_a\): Not all \(\mu\) are equal

  2. Plot the data and assess validity conditions:

par(mfrow = c(2,2))

The experiment aimed to investigate the impact of the sting location on the perceived pain intensity. It is hypothesized that repeated stinging by the individual would potentially elevate their pain tolerance, resulting in varying levels of pain for initial stings.

The graphical representations reveal differences in variability among the distinct groups. The discrepancies can be linked to the limited sample size, as each group comprised only three data points. In the residual vs fitted plot, the disparities in spreads are observed across different body parts. The Scale-Location plot further shows variability differences among the groups, indicating a deviation from the expected assumptions.

Examining the Q-Q plot, it is evident that the majority of data points align well with the reference line, with some deviations at the extreme values. Consequently, there is no apparent issue with normality in the dataset.
